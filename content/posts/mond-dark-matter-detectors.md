Three separate experiments, on three continents, run by three different international collaborations, dug underground to find the same thing. LUX-ZEPLIN in South Dakota. XENONnT in Italy. PandaX-4T in Sichuan, China. The largest, most sensitive dark matter detectors ever built. Between them: hundreds of researchers, decades of work, hundreds of millions of dollars in liquid xenon buried under mountains to get away from cosmic noise.

All three found solar neutrinos.

Not dark matter - the sun. Boron-8 neutrinos streaming through the rock, through the shielding, through the xenon, leaving the faint traces the experiments were built to catch for something else. At nearly identical statistical significance: 2.73 sigma, 2.64 sigma, below the five-sigma discovery threshold, confirming each other's solar neutrino detections.

The universe answered. They asked for dark matter and it sent them the sun.

The three experiments issued press releases framing their results as "world-leading limits" and "narrowing the search." This is true. LZ set the most precise constraints on WIMP cross-sections in history, excluding a huge swath of the parameter space where physicists had predicted the most popular dark matter candidates should be. That is real scientific work.

What neither the press releases nor any major physics body has stated is the thing worth stating: at what point does "narrowing the search" become "the thing you were searching for isn't there"? What evidence would be sufficient to answer that question?

Nobody has said.

---

The LHC failed to find supersymmetric particles. SUSY - a shadow zoo of partner particles predicted by theoretical work since the 1970s - was the dominant expectation for what would appear alongside the Higgs. Beautiful, elegant theory, fills known gaps in the Standard Model. Then the Higgs appeared at 125 GeV, alone, and SUSY partners were not found at any energy the machine could probe.

The community updated. Badly, slowly, expensively - with 500 papers about a phantom particle in 2015 and a 21-billion-euro proposal for a larger collider that even its supporters describe as "like the LHC but without the Higgs." The updating looked like buying a bigger shovel rather than questioning whether you had the right field. But the updating was real. You do not find physicists arguing that SUSY is definitely there and the LHC just didn't look hard enough in the same confident tones as 2010. The parameters shifted. The enthusiasm cooled.

Now ask the same question about WIMPs.

The canonical WIMP mass range - the 10 to 1000 GeV window where theoretical models predicted the most compelling dark matter candidates - has been progressively excluded by increasingly sensitive detectors for forty years. Each experiment set stronger limits than the last. The exclusion region now covers most of the territory that motivated the original proposal. What has not happened is anything resembling the SUSY acknowledgment: a community-wide recognition that this specific form of dark matter is probably not here.

Instead, the hypothesis shifts. WIMPs below 3 GeV (different detection strategy). WIMPs above 10 TeV (indirect detection). Axions, not WIMPs. Primordial black holes. "Fuzzy" ultralight dark matter. Each null result opens new parameter space rather than closing the question. This is not fraud and it is not conspiracy. It is a hypothesis structured such that null results are always reinterpreted as "we need a better detector" rather than "we need a different hypothesis."

What I am looking for, and cannot find, is a formal statement from any major physics body - the American Physical Society, the International Astronomical Union, the Royal Astronomical Society - specifying what evidence would cause the community to abandon the WIMP hypothesis the same way accumulated LHC data caused it to abandon the most popular SUSY predictions. Not "dark matter doesn't exist" - that is a different question. Specifically: what would falsify this particular candidate?

Nobody has said. I checked.

---

While particle physicists were building better xenon detectors, someone else was collecting a different kind of evidence.

MOND - Modified Newtonian Dynamics - was proposed by Mordehai Milgrom in 1983. The core idea: below a specific acceleration scale, Newtonian gravity doesn't work as advertised. Instead of F = ma, you get a modified relation that produces flat galaxy rotation curves without invoking invisible matter.

The proposal came from observation and was immediately productive. From the modified dynamics, Milgrom derived that galaxy rotation speeds should scale with the fourth root of baryonic mass - the baryonic Tully-Fisher relation. He also predicted a specific correlation between observed acceleration and the acceleration calculated from visible matter alone. These were not descriptions of known data. They were predictions.

None of them were confirmed observations when Milgrom made them. They were confirmed afterward.

The third prediction - the correlation between observed and baryonic acceleration - is called the Radial Acceleration Relation. In 2016, McGaugh, Lelli, and Schombert tested it against 175 galaxies with high-quality data. It held, with scatter so tight that most of it was measurement error. In 2024, Haubner and colleagues extended the database to roughly 4,000 galaxies - 23 times larger. The relation held across all of them. Earlier the same year, Mistele and colleagues tested it using gravitational lensing, a completely independent measurement method, reaching 2.5 decades lower in acceleration than kinematic data can probe. The relation held there too.

The external field effect is the strangest prediction. In MOND, a galaxy's internal dynamics depend on its external gravitational environment - a fundamentally non-Newtonian claim with no analog in dark matter models. Chae and colleagues tested this in 2020 and 2021 against roughly 300 galaxies from the SPARC database. They found it.

This is what the other hypothesis's four decades looked like: predictions made before observation, confirmed repeatedly, extended to more data, confirmed again by independent methods.

MOND doesn't work everywhere.

Galaxy clusters require additional mass two to five times beyond visible baryons that MOND doesn't account for. Dark matter would provide this naturally; MOND requires either an exotic particle - probably sterile neutrinos - or an unresolved extension. The CMB acoustic peaks, the fingerprint of early universe density fluctuations, are not naturally reproduced by standard MOND; it needs a relativistic extension to attempt this. The main relativistic extension of MOND, TeVeS, was effectively ruled out in 2017 when gravitational wave observations showed gravity and light travel at the same speed - something TeVeS predicted differently. A newer framework, AeST gravity developed by Skordis and Zlosnik, was proposed to handle this, but it is new and incompletely tested.

Low-mass dwarf galaxies show a deviation from the Radial Acceleration Relation that a 2025 paper in Astronomy & Astrophysics was honest enough to flag without resolving.

I am not telling you MOND is right. The failures are real. A framework that fails in galaxy clusters and doesn't have a clean relativistic extension is not a finished theory.

What I am telling you is that one hypothesis has been making novel predictions that hold up, and the other hypothesis has been accumulating null results in its primary detection strategy for forty years. These two situations are not being treated symmetrically.

The asymmetry is not in the evidence. It is in the epistemology.

Physics has a concept called falsifiability - the requirement that a scientific hypothesis specify, in advance, what observations would prove it wrong. A hypothesis that can never be proven wrong by any possible evidence is not functioning as science; it is functioning as metaphysics with equations. The standard is imperfect in practice. It is the organizing principle anyway.

The WIMP hypothesis, as currently practiced, is structured asymmetrically with respect to this standard. When LHC null results accumulated on SUSY, the community began - slowly, reluctantly - to treat the original parameter space as excluded. When WIMP null results accumulate, the standard response is parameter expansion: maybe we should look somewhere different. The hypothesis is never wrong; it is always preliminary.

There is a reason MOND proponents keep losing the institutional argument despite accumulating evidence. MOND's failures in clusters are treated as decisive - they suggest a fundamental problem. WIMP's failures in direct detection are treated as progress - they narrow the search. Both of these framings might be correct. But the asymmetry is doing work that nobody has had to justify out loud.

The funding tells you where the asymmetry lives. Rough estimates put dark matter research (primarily direct detection) at roughly $130 million per year internationally. MOND research - primarily theoretical and computational, because there is no funded MOND experiment analogous to LZ - runs to maybe half a million dollars per year. Approximately 260 to one. In case you were wondering what the field thinks of this debate.

The 4,000 galaxies following the Radial Acceleration Relation didn't get a press release. LZ setting new limits on a cross-section that may not exist got one.

---

When LZ, XENONnT, and PandaX all detected solar boron-8 neutrinos at the same statistical significance, they were confirming that their detectors work and that they've reached the sensitivity level where solar neutrinos are an irreducible background. At current sensitivities, the "neutrino fog" - the noise floor where solar and atmospheric neutrinos start mimicking potential dark matter signals - begins to limit how much further you can go without dramatically larger detectors.

The next generation proposal, the XLZD consortium combining LZ and XENONnT into a 40-60 tonne detector, is in planning. This will extend the search another order of magnitude or two in sensitivity. More of the parameter space will be excluded. Either a WIMP will appear, which would be transformative and conclusive, or the exclusion region will expand again, and the hypothesis will shift again.

The field knows how to update. We watched it happen with SUSY. The question - and I am not sure anyone has a clean answer to this - is what the updating mechanism looks like for dark matter. SUSY was abandoned because the LHC probed the specific energy scales where theorists had predicted SUSY partners, found nothing, and the theoretical motivation eroded. The theoretical motivation for WIMPs of a specific mass came partly from SUSY's other predictions - the "WIMP miracle" depended on a particle that conveniently had both the right mass and the right cross-section to explain dark matter. If SUSY is gone, the miracle loses its theoretical support. The case for a 100 GeV WIMP is weaker now than it was in 2010. The field has not said this clearly.

At what point does "the search continues" become dishonest framing? Not because the dark matter hypothesis is wrong - there are genuinely good gravitational reasons to expect non-baryonic matter of some kind. But because a hypothesis that generates forty years of null results in its primary detection mode, that has lost the theoretical scaffolding that originally motivated the specific mass range being searched, that competes with an alternative framework accumulating correct predictions in the exact regime where it fails - that hypothesis is not in the same epistemic position it was in 1984.

Nobody has said when the position changes.

That's the silence.
