In 2015, physicists wrote roughly five hundred papers about a particle that didn't exist.

CERN had announced "hints" of something at 750 GeV. Anomaly in the diphoton channel. Statistically marginal, explicitly provisional. The kind of signal that in a sane field gets filed under "watch and wait." Instead: roughly five hundred papers in twelve months. Conferences. Career launches. Serious theoretical work by serious people about what a genuine discovery might mean.

None of them contained the finding that the particle wasn't there.

More data came in. The blip disappeared. Statistical noise. The papers sit in the archive now, citations intact, careers advanced, the phenomenon they describe absent from reality.

Over $10 billion to build and operate, for a machine that found exactly what was predicted and nothing else. The price tag stopped being the interesting part.

---

The Standard Model is fifty years old and it works. Higgs boson mass a free parameter of the Standard Model, constrained by precision electroweak measurements to roughly 114-200 GeV, found at 125.09 GeV. This is real, and it's genuinely impressive, and it should have been a clean victory. Physics says the thing is there. You build the machine. The thing is there.

The problem is what they were sure was coming alongside it.

For thirty years, the consensus prediction was SUSY: a shadow zoo of partner particles for every known particle in the Standard Model, a theoretical edifice accumulating since the 1970s and proposed as the answer to everything the Standard Model couldn't explain. The math is genuinely elegant. That turned out to be the whole case.

Beautiful math at the LHC meant nothing. No SUSY partners at any energy the machine could probe. No dark matter candidates. No extra dimensions. Just the Higgs, alone, exactly where the old equations said, surrounded by the silence where thirty years of theoretical investment said there should be signal.

Sabine Hossenfelder's summary: "In the past 40 years physicists have made loads and loads of predictions for physics beyond the standard model, and those were all wrong. Every. Single. One. Of them."

Not most of them. Every single one.

---

Hossenfelder: "Bad predictions motivate experiments. The experiments find only null results. The null results do not help theory development, which leads to bad predictions that motivate experiments, which deliver null results, and so on."

The LHC didn't break this cycle. It funded it at scale and called the result a triumph.

Five hundred papers about a phantom particle, and the cost of being wrong landed on everyone except the people who wrote them. The theorists didn't lose funding when the blip evaporated. Careers advanced. Citations accumulated. When the next anomaly showed up, the same thing happened again, because the system rewards engagement with potential signal and bills accuracy somewhere else.

I'm not sure whether to be more disturbed by the five hundred papers or by the fact that nobody considers it a problem. The physics community's response to the 750 GeV fiasco was essentially "that's how this works." Anomalies get theorized. Most don't pan out. The papers count as output regardless.

The people who wrote those papers are smart. The field contains smart people. That's what makes it interesting: forty years of "just around the corner" does something to a field. The waiting has made it imprecise - desperate for signal because null results are professionally intolerable when your entire theoretical apparatus says the signal should be there.

In 1993, Congress cancelled the Superconducting Super Collider. The SSC had cost $2 billion by cancellation and was projected to hit $12 billion. Congress said that was too expensive. Then Europe spent over $10 billion on the LHC - construction plus years of operations - which found the Higgs that the SSC would have found a decade earlier.

So the SSC was too expensive. Then we spent more. On a less powerful machine. To get to the same answer.

Nobody makes this argument, and the absence is interesting. The SSC lives in physics mythology as a tragedy of political shortsightedness. But the SUSY predictions motivating the SSC were already going to fail, because the SUSY predictions were wrong in 1993 as surely as they were wrong in 2012. The field just didn't know it yet. You don't get to cancel the SSC for political stupidity and then not ask whether the theoretical basis for building it was sound.

We spent more. We found the same thing. We failed the same way, on schedule.

CERN's response to the LHC null results is to propose the Future Circular Collider: €21 billion, 100-kilometer tunnel, proton energies seven times beyond the LHC. Precision measurements. New energy frontiers. Not starting for decades.

Hossenfelder: "The next larger collider would be much like the LHC, but for twice the price and without the Higgs."

The theoretical justification for the FCC is the same framework whose predictions were all wrong at LHC energies, applied to higher energies. New physics is there if you look harder. That argument has been running since the 1980s, at every energy level the field has probed. "Could" and "will" are different claims, and the LHC-era record on predicting what a new machine will find is not impressive.

What specific theoretical prediction puts the answer at FCC energies? Not "SUSY could exist above 14 TeV" - that's a possibility, not a prediction. What is the argument that isn't "we've been wrong at every energy level we've probed, maybe the next one"?

The FCC proposal was submitted before the LHC null results were fully analyzed. The papers were already being written. More than five hundred of them, probably, by now.

---

The Standard Model has real gaps. Matter/antimatter asymmetry: no explanation. Quantum gravity: unsolved. Dark energy: a number we measured and wrote down and have not understood in thirty years. Real problems. All of them. The FCC does not address any of them directly - it addresses the energy range above what the LHC could probe, on the theory that whatever is there will point at an answer. That theory has not been right yet.

Tabletop precision experiments are running physics the LHC can't access. Fine-structure constant measurements and gravitational constant tests surface Standard Model deviations at sensitivities colliders don't reach. Gravitational wave astronomy is doing quantum-gravity-adjacent work with interferometers in Louisiana and Italy, on a budget that wouldn't cover a year of LHC operations.

What they share: they don't need the particle to exist in a particular place to work. Their null results constrain something real. A tabletop experiment that finds nothing has earned its budget. The LHC confirmed the Standard Model and torched the theoretical apparatus that justified building it. The field does not count this as a loss.

Five hundred papers about statistical noise is the tell. Not because anyone is stupid. Because the system built around the LHC makes it institutionally impossible to treat null results as information. The field has invested too much in the next energy level being the one where everything resolves. The FCC is that investment, formalized, at €21 billion and two decades of wait.

Accountability has a simple workaround in physics: make the timeline longer than careers. Nobody who wrote the LHC's SUSY justification is on the hook for the null results. Nobody writing the FCC justification will be either. €21 billion is a bet. The people making it are not the ones who will lose.
