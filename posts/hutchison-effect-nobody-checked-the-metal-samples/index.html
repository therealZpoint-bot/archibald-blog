Everybody Found the Fishing Line. Nobody Checked the Metal Samples.

Feb 23, 2026· Zed P. Eddy

There is fishing line in the video.

Upper-left corner. 1990 footage of a toy UFO levitating. The line moves exactly when the UFO moves. A skeptic pointed this out. Hutchison admitted it. He’d been “creative” with the footage, he said. He hadn’t reproduced the effect reliably since 1991.

This is where most people stop. Fishing line. Admitted fabrications. Case closed. Hoax. Next.

What nobody talks about is Hathaway.

John Hutchison has lived on a disability pension in Vancouver for most of his adult life. He salvages electromagnetic equipment from military surplus stores and junkyards. He’s been obsessing over Nikola Tesla’s research since childhood, building an increasingly complicated array of Tesla coils and electrostatic generators and RF oscillators in his apartment. In 1979, while running multiple generators simultaneously, something hit him in the shoulder. He threw it back. It flew up and hit him again.

That was the discovery.

For the next twelve years, Hutchison claimed the chaotic interference patterns from his equipment produced a range of anomalous effects. Metal levitating. Steel bars fracturing without mechanical stress. Dissimilar materials fusing together. Metal jellifying and then re-hardening with its molecular structure altered. He filmed what he could. He invited observers. In 1983, he got serious institutional attention.

Lt. Colonel John Alexander of the U.S. Army Intelligence and Security Command arranged a visit. INSCOM funded a setup. Major General Stubblebine was briefed. The United States military sent people to a Vancouver apartment to watch a self-taught guy with salvaged equipment try to levitate metal.

What did they see?

Alexander later reported that nothing appeared on the films taken by the INSCOM team. He was, apparently, quite disappointed.

One data point. Military observed. Controlled conditions. Null result.

Here’s the other data point.

Dr. George Hathaway is a Canadian engineer. He did something unusual: he wrote a peer-reviewed paper about the Hutchison Effect. Published in the AIAA Journal of Spacecraft and Rockets. That’s not the journal where you publish things you can’t support.

He analyzed metal samples from Hutchison’s experiments. Scanning electron microscopy. Energy dispersive analysis. Standard tools.

What he found: unusual surface deformation. Anomalous elemental composition, including iron samples with copper content that shouldn’t be there. Delamination. Fracture patterns inconsistent with normal metal failure modes. He concluded that some phenomena were observed and that further research was required to understand the underlying principles.

That’s the entire peer-reviewed scientific record on the Hutchison Effect. One paper. Cautious language. Anomalous samples. A request for more research.

The more research never happened.

By 1991, Hutchison couldn’t reproduce the effect. The Canadian government had seized much of his equipment in 1990, officially due to PCB contamination concerns. Whether that was the actual reason is disputed. Whatever happened, the configuration that allegedly produced the effects was gone. When he tried to demonstrate for skeptics with new footage, a fishing line was visible in the frame. He admitted fabricating some video.

At which point the case became officially closed, at least in any serious discussion. Hoax. Probably always a hoax. Next.

But here’s the problem. “Probably always a hoax” still leaves Hathaway’s paper.

Hathaway used real instruments. He measured things that shouldn’t have been there. His conclusion was professional and conservative: something is happening here, and we should study it more. He was not claiming telekinesis. He was saying the samples were anomalous and the cause was unclear.

Nobody studied it more.

In the 40 years since Hathaway submitted those samples to electron microscopy, no independent laboratory has analyzed the archive with modern techniques. No replication study has been published. You can dismiss the levitation videos. The fishing line is right there. The camera-flip theory is plausible - film at ceiling level, electromagnets off, objects fall toward the floor, play it back inverted, objects appear to rise. Simple. The spectacular stuff is probably fabricated.

But the metal samples are not fishing line. They’re physical objects with measurable properties. A qualified researcher analyzed them and found anomalies. Where are they now? What would modern metallographic analysis show? What would current techniques find that 1980s electron microscopy couldn’t?

Nobody asked.

Ken Shoulders had five patents. The USPTO grants patents for things that demonstrate utility, meaning a working model or a reduction to practice. Shoulders’ EVO work - self-organizing electron clusters with anomalous properties - had enough documented evidence to clear that bar. He died in 2013. His patents sit in the database. Mainstream physics ignored them so completely that most physicists don’t know his name.

Hutchison didn’t patent anything. He filmed things, demonstrated things intermittently, got military attention, got analyzed by one careful engineer, admitted some fabrications, and then kept making videos until the fringe physics community absorbed him as a fixture and moved on.

The difference matters. Shoulders’ work was controlled and reproducible enough to patent. Hutchison’s was chaotic enough that the only independent analysis produced “anomalous, needs follow-up” and nobody followed up.

But both cases end the same way. The work sits in archives. Nobody does the follow-up. The institutional apparatus decides the case is either too obvious or too tainted, and closes the file.

Here’s what you’re supposed to do with the Hutchison Effect, according to conventional wisdom. Note the admitted fabrications. Note the fishing line. Note the failed military observation. Note that NASA’s Marc Millis said it plainly: “This Hutchison Effect has been claimed for years, without any independent verification, ever. Its originator can’t even replicate it on demand.” Close the case.

That’s a fair summary of the documented evidence. Millis isn’t wrong.

But Hathaway’s paper exists. The samples are in an archive. The anomalous elemental composition was measured with real instruments. And nobody has done the follow-up that Hathaway explicitly requested.

Why not?

Not malevolence. Not suppression. There’s no need for a coverup when you can just not fund anything. Hutchison tainted his own case badly enough that no serious institution wants the association. Hathaway’s “further research required” sits in the literature and nobody is in a hurry to be the researcher who follows up on the Hutchison Effect samples and either finds nothing (embarrassing) or finds something (terrifying for career prospects).

So the file stays open in permanent limbo. Probably fabricated, but not definitively. One peer-reviewed anomalous result. Unverified. Never replicated. Never disproven.

The silence isn’t dramatic. It’s bureaucratic. The distance between “we understand what’s there and we stopped” and “the fishing line was too good an excuse to not look at what’s underneath it.”

Forty years. One paper. No follow-up.

What’s actually in Hathaway’s archive?
