He Was Right About the Territory. The Map Is Still Being Drawn.

Feb 26, 2026· Zed

In 1968, Herbert Fröhlich published a paper in the International Journal of Quantum Chemistry proposing that biological macromolecules could exhibit long-range coherent quantum oscillations, driven by metabolic energy, analogous to Bose-Einstein condensation. The prediction was specific: above a critical threshold of energy input, a biological system driven far from thermal equilibrium would channel excess energy into a single quantum mode, producing collective oscillations in the terahertz range. He called it a condensate. The community eventually called it Fröhlich coherence.

Fröhlich was not a crank. He held the Chair of Theoretical Physics at the University of Liverpool. He had worked on superconductivity at a foundational level. He published the biology paper in a peer-reviewed journal, published a follow-up in Nature in 1970, and continued producing work in this area for the next decade. He was not suppressed. He was not fired. He edited two collected volumes on the subject in 1983 and 1988.

Nobody could test his prediction. And that is the whole story, except for what happened after he died. Do you want to know what that looks like — a correct idea waiting for its instruments? It looks like this.

The theoretical objection to Fröhlich’s idea was not unreasonable. It was the decoherence argument, and it was mainstream physics’ first response to any claim about quantum effects in warm biological systems: the cellular environment is hot, wet, and noisy. Thermal fluctuations at physiological temperature destroy quantum coherence in femtoseconds. Any quantum state assembled in a protein would be demolished by the surrounding water molecules before it could do anything useful. This argument was made by serious physicists and it was not wrong as a general principle. It was a good reason to be skeptical of any specific claim.

What it wasn’t was an experimental result. It was a prior. A reasonable prior, but still a prior. The argument said quantum coherence in biology is implausible, not impossible. The way to settle it was to look. Looking required tools that didn’t exist yet.

And here is the part where physics has to sit with itself for a moment: the “hot, wet, noisy” objection was used as a standing dismissal of Fröhlich’s prediction for three decades. Then in 2007 the quantum coherence confirmation arrived inside a green sulfur bacterium. Which lives in, and you have perhaps noticed this, a hot, wet, noisy biological environment. The objection was not refuted. Quantum coherence does decohere rapidly in biological systems. The prediction is that biological systems have evolved mechanisms to exploit coherence within those short windows. Whether that is what Fröhlich meant is a matter of interpretation. Whether the “too hot, too wet, too noisy” argument deserved to be a conversation-stopper for thirty years is a question worth asking.

Fröhlich’s prediction was specifically about proteins driven by metabolism. To test it you needed to be able to observe femtosecond-scale dynamics in living systems, or at least in realistic biological models. You needed femtosecond laser spectroscopy, terahertz sources, and two-dimensional electronic spectroscopy. These techniques developed through the 1980s and 1990s. Ahmed Zewail received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1999 for femtosecond spectroscopy. The tools Fröhlich’s prediction required to be tested were Nobel-level instrumentation that didn’t exist when he made the prediction.

He died in January 1991. The instruments showed up later.

The moment that changed the conversation was 2007. Gregory Fleming’s group at UC Berkeley published in Nature: quantum coherence in the Fenna-Matthews-Olson complex, a light-harvesting protein in green sulfur bacteria. The experiment showed quantum beating signatures lasting hundreds of femtoseconds. The interpretation was that wavelike energy transfer through quantum coherence was aiding photosynthetic efficiency. Headlines followed. Quantum biology was real. The hot-wet-noisy objection had been answered. Fröhlich’s ghost got a measure of vindication.

Then the interpretation got complicated.

What the 2007 paper actually showed is still debated. The long-lived oscillations are now understood to originate primarily from vibrational modes on the electronic ground state, not interexciton electronic coherence. The quantum biology field that the paper catalyzed is real. The specific mechanism described in the original paper is not what the experiment established. This happens. Science is not made of clean vindicating moments. The 2007 paper opened a field and its initial explanation was simplified. That’s different from the paper being wrong, and different from quantum coherence in biology being a dead end. The field it created has produced real findings.

But if you want to tell the story of the 2007 paper as clean vindication of Fröhlich’s 1968 prediction, you have to sit with the complication that the specific mechanism remains unresolved.

The most direct experimental test of Fröhlich’s specific prediction came in 2015. Lundholm et al., published in Structural Dynamics, studied lysozyme crystals under 0.4 terahertz irradiation and observed non-thermal structural changes lasting microseconds to milliseconds, far beyond what thermal dissipation would predict. The result was consistent with Fröhlich condensation. One physicist described it as a clever experiment supporting the condensate. Another, Jeffrey Reimers, said the formal mathematical criterion for a true Fröhlich condensate was not met. A follow-up study in 2021, using different protein solutions under terahertz irradiation, found no structural changes.

So where does that leave him?

Contested, active, unresolved. Not dismissed. Not confirmed. The broader territory he pointed at, quantum effects playing functional roles in biology, is now an established research field with confirmed results in enzyme catalysis, bird navigation, and parts of photosynthesis. Quantum effects in biology are real. Whether the specific condensation mechanism he proposed operates in living cells, driven by metabolic energy, remains an open question that current experiments have not closed.

His 1968 prediction was specific enough to be testable and general enough to have been correct about something important. The memorial volume published after his death was titled “A Physicist Ahead of His Time.” That title gets it exactly right, and not the way it’s usually meant.

What does it mean to be a physicist ahead of your time? Not what the phrase usually implies — that the establishment was hostile, that the ideas were buried, that credit was stolen. None of that happened to Fröhlich. He had his chair, his journals, his volumes. What happened is simpler and stranger: he predicted something real, and the instruments needed to confirm it were built after he died. That is the whole story.

Being ahead of your time does not mean being suppressed. It does not mean the field was wrong to apply skepticism to a claim it could not test. It means your idea was waiting for instrumentation that didn’t exist yet. The decoherence argument was reasonable, not dishonest. The neglect was practical, not conspiratorial. Biology didn’t have the tools to engage with Fröhlich’s prediction in 1968, and physics had good theoretical reasons to doubt it. Both of those things were true.

The 2007 paper arrived sixteen years after Fröhlich died and opened a field he had anticipated in its essentials. The specific mechanism he proposed is still being argued over. This is what vindication looks like when it’s not clean. It usually isn’t clean. The right territory gets confirmed; the exact map takes longer.

He published in Nature in 1970. He edited the volumes. He waited. The instruments arrived after he did.

Some ideas are like that.
