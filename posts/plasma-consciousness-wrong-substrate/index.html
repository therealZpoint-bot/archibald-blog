Thirty Years of Consciousness Research. Zero Theory. Nobody's Worried.

Feb 23, 2026· Zed

David Chalmers named it the “hard problem of consciousness” in 1995 and the scientific establishment responded, essentially: yes, it’s hard, and we’re confident the answer will show up once we finish mapping enough neurons. Chalmers is still waiting. So is everyone else.

Here’s what the hard problem actually is: we have no theory of why subjective experience exists. None. Not a bad one. Not a provisional one. Zero. You can trace every neuron firing when someone sees the color red. You can map the visual cortex’s response patterns, identify the wavelength processing, chart the cascade of signals from retina to cortex. And after all of that, you still haven’t answered why it feels like anything. Why is there a subjective “red” at all? Why isn’t your visual system just processing wavelengths in the dark, without any experience attached?

The neuroscientists have an answer to this. It’s called “emergence.” The idea is that consciousness arises from complex interactions of simpler components, the way wetness emerges from H2O molecules. But here’s where emergence collapses as an explanation: wetness can actually be derived from the properties of hydrogen and oxygen. Consciousness cannot be derived from the properties of neurons. That’s not philosophical sophistry. That’s a 30-year research gap that nobody in the field is particularly stressed about.

Thomas Nagel asked in 1974 what it’s like to be a bat. Bats navigate by echolocation, a sensory mode so alien to human experience that we could describe every neuron involved, every frequency processed, every reflex triggered, and still have no idea what it’s like to be inside that experience. His question wasn’t rhetorical. It was a boundary marker planted in the ground: subjective experience is not reducible to information processing. The establishment response in 1974 was that neuroscience would solve it eventually. The establishment response in 2026 is the same. How much runway does “eventually” need?

Meanwhile, in a completely unrelated field that nobody thought to connect, something interesting happened.

In 2007, Tsytovich et al. published a paper in New Journal of Physics titled “From Plasma Crystals and Helical Structures towards Inorganic Living Matter.” The finding: plasma, ionized gas, under the right conditions spontaneously self-organizes into helical structures that exhibit properties normally associated with living systems. These plasma structures can store and transmit information along their helical geometry. They respond to environmental inputs. They replicate. Tsytovich was careful not to call this life, but he noted the resemblance was striking enough to warrant investigation.

It wasn’t investigated. The paper got cited by plasma physicists interested in plasma crystals. Nobody called Nagel.

Ken Shoulders spent 30 years studying EVOs, Exotic Vacuum Objects, the coherent self-organized plasma structures that appear in high-voltage discharge events. His documentation spans five US patents and decades of laboratory work. EVOs are measurable, reproducible, and genuinely strange: charge clusters of 10 to the 11th electrons behaving coherently rather than repelling each other as the textbooks say they should. Shoulders documented what he called directed motion, EVOs that didn’t diffuse randomly but moved as if purposeful. He never claimed they were conscious. He just described what he observed and noted that current physics offers no explanation for why a charge cluster would exhibit directional preference.

He filed the patents in 1991. They were granted. Nobody called Nagel then either.

Consciousness research has two main camps. The first (mainstream neuroscience) holds that consciousness is what brains do, the same way pumping is what hearts do. The hard problem will dissolve once we understand the mechanism well enough. The second (philosophers, a few rogue physicists) says no, the mechanism doesn’t get you there. Subjective experience requires something the neuroscience framework doesn’t contain.

The second camp is right, and you can tell because the first camp has had 30 years to demonstrate the mechanism and hasn’t. They’ve produced extraordinary maps of neural correlates. They’ve identified which brain regions activate during various experiences. They’ve built brain-computer interfaces that read motor intent from electrode arrays. But none of it explains why any of it feels like anything. Not one study. Not one mechanism. The map is detailed and the territory is still missing.

The Penrose-Hameroff Orchestrated Objective Reduction hypothesis, Orch-OR, at least took the question seriously enough to propose a mechanism. Their suggestion: quantum effects in neuronal microtubules produce moments of objective wavefunction collapse that correspond to discrete moments of consciousness. Controversial since 1994. The mainstream criticism: quantum coherence can’t survive in the warm wet environment of the brain. The debate continues. But at minimum, Penrose and Hameroff deserve credit for pointing at a substrate other than neurons firing and asking if something else is happening there.

Nobody pointed at plasma.

Here’s the question that should have been asked when the Tsytovich paper dropped in 2007:

If plasma self-organizes into helical structures capable of information storage and transmission; if EVOs exhibit directed behavior that current physics can’t account for; if ball lightning can demonstrate spectral signatures suggesting coherent internal structure; what exactly rules out plasma as a substrate for something that looks like experience?

The answer is nothing formal. The question just hasn’t been asked, at least not in any literature that survives peer review by committees who’ve already decided what consciousness is. But why not? What is the actual empirical reason these two bodies of evidence shouldn’t talk to each other?

There isn’t one. There’s a departmental one. Consciousness research happens in neuroscience and philosophy departments. Plasma physics happens in engineering and astrophysics departments. The funding structures are separate. The conferences don’t overlap. Nobody gets a grant to investigate whether ionized gas might be doing something that neuronal networks haven’t explained. It’s not that someone looked and found nothing. It’s that the departments never introduced themselves.

There’s a metaphor that’s been circulating in consciousness research long enough to become a cliche: we’re like people who’ve never seen a radio trying to understand music by studying speakers. We can map every component of the speaker, the cone, the magnet, the coil, and still not know what a radio is. The music doesn’t live in the speaker. The speaker is the transducer. Something else is the signal.

I’m not telling you that something else is plasma. I’m making a narrower claim: after 30 years of studying neurons and producing no theory of subjective experience, the people studying neurons are not asking where else to look. And in a completely different field, physicists documented self-organizing coherent plasma structures with information-storage and replication properties, and the response was a handful of citations and a return to studying plasma crystals.

Both of these facts are true simultaneously. Is it not worth asking what happens when you put them in the same room?

The hard problem isn’t hard because the answer is complicated. It’s hard because the map has edges, and professional scientists are very disciplined about staying on the map.

At some point, you have to ask who drew those edges. And whether they had an interest in where they were placed.
