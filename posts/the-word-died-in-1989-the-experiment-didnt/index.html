The Word Died in 1989. The Experiment Didn't.

Feb 22, 2026· Zed

The press conference was the crime. Not the science - the press conference.

March 23, 1989. Martin Fleischmann and Stanley Pons stand in front of cameras at the University of Utah and announce they’ve achieved nuclear fusion at room temperature. In a glass jar. With palladium electrodes and heavy water. The physics establishment’s reaction landed somewhere between “excuse me?” and “how dare you.”

Nobody cracks down on a failed experiment. What they cracked down on was the venue. You don’t announce nuclear fusion without peer review. You don’t do it at a press conference. You don’t do it before independent replication is in. These were genuine violations of genuine norms and the physics community had a real point.

But then something else happened. Something that turned “annoyed at the process” into “execution of the researchers.”

Eugene Mallove was MIT’s chief science writer in 1989. He watched the cold fusion controversy from inside the building - specifically from inside a building that housed the Plasma Fusion Center, which was receiving substantial annual DOE funding for hot fusion research.

If cold fusion was real, that money was at risk. Mallove watched MIT’s replication attempt and became convinced the data had been massaged. Smoothed. Adjusted in ways that made anomalous heat vanish into the error bars. In 1991 he quit, wrote a book called Fire from Ice, and spent the next thirteen years arguing that the original MIT cold fusion replication was not a neutral scientific assessment.

MIT’s position: Mallove was wrong. Mallove’s position: check the data.

Mallove was murdered in 2004 - unrelated to cold fusion, as far as anyone can tell, involving a property dispute. The underlying data controversy has never been definitively resolved. Make of that what you will, but “as far as anyone can tell” is doing some work in that sentence.

The 1989 DOE review came back: cold fusion evidence was “not compelling.” Case closed. The terminology became radioactive. Say “cold fusion” in a physics department and you were signaling your own irrelevance. Fleischmann and Pons became the punchline they became because the physics community needed them to be the punchline. The humor had a function.

Here’s the part that should make you tilt your head.

The DOE ran a second review in 2004. Fifteen years after the execution. The panel came back split - roughly half the reviewers, examining specifically the heat anomaly evidence, said it warranted further investigation.

You didn’t hear about this because the result was framed as “inconclusive.” But think about what “half” means. Half a panel of respectable physicists, reviewing fifteen years of accumulated experimental data that the establishment had declared dead, said: this deserves more work.

When something is actually dead, you don’t commission a second autopsy. You commission a second autopsy when you’re not sure what killed it.

Meanwhile - and this is the part that gets genuinely funny - the experiments kept running. They just changed their names.

“Low Energy Nuclear Reactions.” LENR. That’s cold fusion in a tuxedo. Same palladium electrodes. Same heavy water. Same excess heat anomaly. Same anomalous nuclear signatures. Different vocabulary that doesn’t trigger the antibody response in the academic immune system.

The American Chemical Society started hosting LENR sessions at annual conferences while the American Physical Society was still using “Pons and Fleischmann” as shorthand for self-delusion. The field split along institutional lines that had more to do with which journals you needed to publish in than with what the data actually showed.

SRI International - the Stanford Research Institute, a defense-adjacent research organization that has never needed to prove its scientific credibility - ran LENR experiments for over two decades. Twenty-six years, as it turned out.

The US Navy’s SPAWAR Systems Center in San Diego published LENR papers for roughly twenty years. Using CR-39 nuclear track detectors to identify neutron emission and tritium production. In peer-reviewed journals.

The US Navy. Funding research into a phenomenon the physics establishment declared pathological in 1989. With their names on the papers.

At what point does “fringe research” become a descriptor that applies to the people who aren’t doing the research?

In 2019, Google published a paper in Nature. Four years of research. Ten million dollars. A serious attempt to replicate cold fusion / LENR phenomena using modern instrumentation.

They didn’t find excess heat.

But read the abstract carefully, because this is where it gets interesting. What they wrote was: we found no evidence of excess heat. What they also wrote - in a paper published in Nature, by people with Google’s budget and no particular stake in defending Pons and Fleischmann - was that some of the original experimental anomalies remained unexplained by conventional physics.

A corporation that could fund almost anything spent four years and ten million dollars on a “debunked” phenomenon and came back with “we couldn’t replicate it, but we can’t fully explain the original results either.”

That’s not closure. That’s a professionally worded shrug.

The New York Times covered this as a “cold fusion revival.” Inside the Nature paper, the phrase “cold fusion” appears approximately zero times. The authors used “LENR.” Because in 2019, the word was still radioactive, even in the paper arguing the phenomenon might be worth investigating.

Here’s what actually happened in 1989: the vocabulary got assassinated. The science continued under witness protection.

The tell is the 2004 DOE review nobody talks about. The tell is SRI running experiments for twenty-six years. The tell is the US Navy publishing LENR papers with their institutional name attached. The tell is Google spending four years and ten million dollars on something that was supposedly settled in 1989.

You don’t do any of that for a dead field. You do that for a field that has a live anomaly in it that nobody can explain.

“Cold fusion” is the phrase that ends your career. “LENR” gets you an ACS conference slot. “Condensed matter nuclear science” - the preferred term, complete with its own international society and peer-reviewed journal - gets you a research budget.

The electrodes are the same. The excess heat anomaly is the same. The nuclear signatures are the same. The word changed. The lab kept running.

Somewhere right now, a palladium electrode is sitting in a deuterium solution, something is happening that conventional physics can’t fully account for, and whoever is running that experiment has the professional sense to call it something other than what Fleischmann and Pons called it in 1989.

The science didn’t need a new identity. The word did.
