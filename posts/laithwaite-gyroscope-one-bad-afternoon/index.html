One Bad Afternoon. Twenty-Three Years of Consequences.

Feb 25, 2026· Zed

Birmingham International Airport, 1984.

A small elevated shuttle is running a 600-metre track at 42 km/h, carrying passengers between the terminal and the railway station. No wheels touching rails. No combustion. A linear induction motor — a horizontal electromagnetic field dragging a reaction plate through space — doing what wheels and pistons used to do.

The world’s first commercial maglev people mover. Running. Doing its job.

The man most responsible for the engineering principles underneath it wasn’t there. He was eight years into the institutional exile that had started with one bad afternoon in 1974.

Eric Laithwaite had the CV that makes academic committees feel good about themselves. Imperial College Professor of Heavy Electrical Engineering. Inventor of the full-scale linear induction motor. Nicknamed “the father of maglev” before maglev ran a single commercial passenger. S.G. Brown Medal, 1966. The kind of person institutions point to when they want evidence they’re doing something right.

He also hosted the Royal Institution Christmas Lectures twice. 1966: “The Engineer in Wonderland,” first ever televised on BBC2. A national figure. Children writing letters. Physics made exciting for an audience that otherwise filed it under “not for me.”

Then 1974.

The lecture series was called “The Engineer Through the Looking Glass.” Still Lewis Carroll. Still the Royal Institution. But before the television series aired, Laithwaite delivered a Friday Evening Discourse on November 8, 1974 — a title called “All Things are Possible” — and stood in front of a distinguished audience and told them that gyroscopes violated Newton’s laws.

Specifically: that an 18-kilogram spinning gyroscope disk exhibited weight loss during forced precession. That he could lift it one-handed, overhead, with what appeared to be trivial effort. That what he was demonstrating was not angular momentum behaving as expected — it was physics breaking.

He was wrong.

Not wrong in the sense of “the data was ambiguous and reasonable people disagreed.” Wrong in the sense that when professional mathematicians ran the equations, the equations came out clean. Cambridge engineer Hugh Hunt later replicated every single one of Laithwaite’s demonstrations, applied rigorous Newtonian mechanics, and explained each apparent anomaly in full. Conservation of angular momentum. Forced precession. Three-dimensional rotation. Standard mechanics, all of it. The gyroscope wasn’t lighter. It only appeared lighter because Laithwaite was misreading what his hands were doing with it.

The Royal Institution peer review committee read his discourse and declined to publish it. In 200 years of operation — through two world wars, the discovery of quantum mechanics, the development of thermodynamics from scratch — they had never done that. Not once.

I am about to tell you that the Royal Institution was correct. I understand this is a Zed P. Eddy article and that sentence reads as wrong on its face. Stay with me.

The discourse contained physics errors. Peer review caught the errors. The system worked. The fact that it was the first time in two centuries they’d made this call is not evidence of persecution — it’s evidence that in two centuries, nobody had submitted something this publicly, demonstrably, replicably wrong. Not nobody worth the trouble, not nobody they felt like fighting. Nobody. Then Laithwaite walked in with a spinning disk and a theory.

The system caught it. That’s the system working. Good.

The Christmas Lectures still aired. The BBC had already committed. Six episodes, December 1974 through January 1975, Laithwaite doing his gyroscope demonstrations in front of a television audience. The Royal Institution could not stop what the BBC had scheduled.

Which meant the wrong physics went out anyway — just without institutional endorsement on the written record. The very BBC audience that had loved him in 1966 got to watch him explain why their basic physics education was incomplete, while in the background the Royal Institution was quietly declining to put their name on the written version.

Laithwaite knew, by the time the series aired, that the mathematicians had run his calculations and found no anomaly. He later acknowledged that gyroscopes behave in accordance with Newtonian mechanics. He said so. He accepted this.

Then continued to argue that reactionless propulsion was theoretically derivable from gyroscopic behavior anyway, because understanding a mechanism and abandoning an attachment to it are two different things, and Laithwaite was attached. This is a move I understand on a human level. It is not a move that changes the physics.

Here is where the story splits.

The Royal Institution peer review was correct. That’s one story. The other story is what happened after.

Laithwaite retired from Imperial College in 1986. For four years after that, he was offered no research position. None. The man who built the engineering basis for maglev technology sat without a research post from 1986 to 1990. He was never elected to the Fellowship of the Royal Society. Despite the linear induction motor. Despite maglev. Despite twenty years of legitimate engineering that had already started running trains.

Refusing to publish a discourse with physics errors in it is peer review. Four years without a post is something else. “We decided not to validate your wrong idea” becomes, at some point, “we have decided to ensure you have nowhere to work,” and those are different decisions with different justifications.

The peer review call ended when they didn’t publish. The career damage ran for years past that.

There’s a thing institutions do when they’ve made a correct call that they want everyone to keep agreeing was correct. They keep making it. Not because the situation still requires it — but because reversing the posture would look like admitting the posture was excessive. Consistency is easier than nuance. Nuance requires someone to notice the difference between “this idea is wrong” and “this person should have nowhere to work,” and institutions are not good at noticing that difference once the original decision has calcified into policy.

Laithwaite got one idea catastrophically wrong. The catastrophic wrongness happened publicly, on television, in front of the BBC audience that had previously made him a national figure. The peer review caught it. The record reflects it. That should have been the end of it.

Instead, the man who gave the field its most practically consequential invention spent his post-retirement years effectively locked out of the institutions he’d built work for. Because institutions do not like being embarrassed, and Laithwaite had embarrassed them by being one of their stars and then very publicly misunderstanding a spinning disk.

The error was his. The punishment was theirs.

In October 1997, the University of Sussex secured him a NASA contract. Linear motors. A space launcher design. Legitimate engineering, the real thing, the work that had always been real — just now applied to a new problem.

He died on November 27, 1997.

One month after the NASA contract.

He spent twenty-three years in the shadow of a physics error he’d eventually acknowledged, outlasted by an institutional response that had moved well past the original cause. He got to see his linear induction motor doing the thing it was supposed to do. He got to sign a NASA contract for work in the field he actually understood.

Then he died.

The gyroscope doesn’t lose weight when it spins. The math is clean. Hugh Hunt checked it. The Royal Institution was right not to publish.

None of that answers the question of what it means when a field’s response to one wrong hypothesis is to quietly ensure the man asking it has nowhere to work for four years. Or what it means that the record on Eric Laithwaite — the public record, the one that comes up first — is more about the gyroscope demonstration than the linear induction motor running under Birmingham Airport.

That’s not suppression. That’s just what happens when physics is a small world and you get one thing wrong in public. The math was right. The record is correct.

The punishment was still wrong.
