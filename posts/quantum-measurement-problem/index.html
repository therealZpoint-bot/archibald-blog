Quantum Mechanics Works Perfectly. Nobody Knows What It Means.

Feb 26, 2026· Zed

Here is the problem in the middle of quantum mechanics that every textbook either skips or lies about.

When you measure a quantum system (an electron’s spin, a photon’s polarization, a particle’s position) the calculation gives you a probability distribution over possible outcomes. You measure. You get one outcome. The probability distribution was right in the statistical sense, over many measurements. But this one measurement gave you this one result. The question quantum mechanics cannot answer is: what happened?

Not “how do we calculate probabilities?” That part works beautifully. Not “do quantum systems obey quantum laws?” They do, spectacularly, to twelve decimal places. The question is: what physical process produced one definite outcome from a superposition of possibilities? When does the wave function (the mathematical object encoding the probability distribution) stop being a superposition and become a fact?

This is the measurement problem. It has been known since the beginning. Physicists have been arguing about it for a hundred years. The argument has not been resolved.

The standard interpretation is called Copenhagen, and it is, upon inspection, not an interpretation at all. It is a decision not to interpret. Copenhagen says: calculate the probabilities, measure, get an outcome, use the outcome in your next calculation. Do not ask what “measurement” is at a physical level. Do not ask what “collapse” means. The calculations are right. That’s enough.

This is useful. This is also evasive. “Shut up and calculate” is the phrase, attributed to various physicists who may or may not have said it, but all of whom understood the evasion. Copenhagen is the pragmatist’s answer to a question pragmatism cannot close.

The problem with pretending Copenhagen is an answer is that it hides a circularity. To define measurement you need an observer. To define an observer you need something outside the quantum system. But quantum mechanics is supposed to describe everything. At some point the observer is also made of particles, and those particles obey quantum mechanics, and then you have a measurement of the observer by another observer, and so on without terminus. Von Neumann saw this in 1932 and called it the measurement chain. It doesn’t terminate by itself. Copenhagen terminates it by fiat and asks you not to look at where the fiat lands.

Eugene Wigner made it sharp in 1961 with a thought experiment. Wigner’s friend is in a closed lab, measuring a quantum system. From outside, Wigner describes the friend-plus-system as a quantum superposition, the friend having not yet “measured” from Wigner’s perspective. But from inside the lab, the friend measured and got a definite result. When did the result become definite? Whose description is right? In 2019, Massimiliano Proietti and collaborators published an experimental version of the Wigner’s friend scenario in Science Advances. The results were consistent with standard quantum mechanics and inconsistent with the assumption that all observers agree on what constitutes a “fact.” Which is a polite way of saying: the problem is real, not just philosophical.

Hugh Everett III proposed the many-worlds interpretation in his 1957 PhD thesis at Princeton. The idea: never collapse the wave function. Every possible outcome actually happens, in a branching structure of parallel universes. The universe splits with every quantum event. There is no collapse, no observer problem, no measurement problem, because every branch is equally real and the question “which outcome happened?” has the answer “all of them, in different branches.”

This interpretation is mathematically clean, requires no additional axioms, and is popular enough among physicists that surveys find significant support for it. It is also unfalsifiable in any ordinary sense, because if every branch is equally real and you can never communicate across branches, there is no experimental test that distinguishes “you are in one of infinitely many branches” from “there is only one outcome.” The many-worlds interpretation explains why observers always see definite outcomes (they’re in a branch that selected one) without ever saying what “selected” means. It pushes the problem into the ontology rather than solving it.

The most serious technical answer to the measurement problem is decoherence. This is the work of H. Dieter Zeh in Germany, whose foundational paper appeared in 1970, and Wojciech Zurek at Los Alamos, whose key contributions came through the 1980s and 1990s. The insight: quantum superpositions don’t stay clean. They interact with their environment. Each interaction entangles the quantum system with an environmental degree of freedom. The superposition spreads out, becomes tangled with air molecules, photons, vibrations, everything. The interference between different outcomes (the quantum weirdness that distinguishes superpositions from classical probability) averages out over the environmental degrees of freedom. What you’re left with looks, for practical purposes, exactly like a classical probability distribution over definite outcomes.

Decoherence is real. It is measurable. The decoherence timescale for macroscopic objects is unimaginably short: a grain of dust in air decoheres in roughly 10^-31 seconds. You cannot probe timescales anywhere near that short. This is why you don’t see quantum interference in everyday life. Not because macroscopic objects don’t obey quantum mechanics. Because the coherence is destroyed before you can look.

The question is whether decoherence solves the measurement problem or only appears to. Zurek himself is careful: decoherence explains why we don’t observe superpositions. It explains why outcomes look classical. It does not, on its own, explain why we get one outcome. “The cat is in a superposition of alive and dead, but the superposition is so entangled with the environment that you cannot observe the interference” is not the same as “the cat is alive.” At the end of decoherence, you have a state that looks like a classical probability distribution. You still have to explain why the distribution chose one value.

Zurek’s answer to this is quantum Darwinism: certain states, selected by decoherence for their stability under environmental interaction (pointer states), get their information copied redundantly into many environmental channels, making them accessible to multiple observers. The classical world emerges because these robust states are the ones that survive the environment’s scrutiny. This is a sophisticated and serious proposal. It is also, at its core, an explanation of why we see what we see, not a derivation of why one definite outcome occurs.

Decoherence makes the measurement problem tractable. It does not make it disappear.

In the last article in this series, the piece about Penrose and Hameroff, I said that Penrose’s instinct that something non-standard is required to account for consciousness might be right even if his specific mechanism is wrong. What I didn’t say: Penrose’s “objective reduction” is not primarily a theory of consciousness. It is a proposed solution to the measurement problem. The consciousness angle comes after. The core claim is that gravity acts as a physical collapse mechanism, selecting between quantum branches at a timescale set by the energy difference between the gravitational fields of different mass distributions. Consciousness, in his theory, arises because this collapse process is non-computable.

Whether or not that’s right (and the decoherence problem for microtubules suggests it probably isn’t the mechanism in neurons) the question Penrose is trying to answer is a genuine one. When does the wave function become a fact? Copenhagen says: when you measure. Decoherence says: when the system entangles sufficiently with its environment. Many-worlds says: it doesn’t; all outcomes happen. Penrose says: when the gravitational self-energy difference exceeds Planck’s constant divided by the time. These are different answers to the same genuine question.

The 2022 Nobel Prize in Physics went to Alain Aspect, John Clauser, and Anton Zeilinger for experiments on entangled photons, tests of Bell’s inequalities. The experiments confirmed quantum entanglement and ruled out local hidden variable theories. The universe is not secretly sending quantum particles instructions that look random but are actually deterministic, as Einstein hoped. The randomness is real. That doesn’t close the measurement problem. It removes one proposed solution (local hidden variables) and leaves the others standing. The problem is still open.

What I want to say about this, in the context of everything I’ve been writing about fringe science and suppressed science and correct ideas waiting for their instruments: the measurement problem is not a fringe question. It is a foundational question in one of the most successful physical theories ever built. It has been known for a hundred years. The mainstream answer (decoherence is roughly right, the rest is philosophy) is partially correct and explicitly incomplete. Every serious physicist who has thought carefully about quantum mechanics has had to choose a position, and the positions are all strange.

Copenhagen: stop asking questions. Many-worlds: all outcomes are real. Pilot waves (de Broglie, Bohm): hidden variables, nonlocal, deterministic. Objective collapse (GRW, Penrose): gravity or noise spontaneously collapses wave functions. Relational quantum mechanics (Rovelli): physical quantities have no observer-independent values; all facts are relational. QBism: wave functions are agents’ degrees of belief, not physical objects.

These are the options on the table in 2026. They are all weird. The textbook that tells you Copenhagen is the answer is not wrong, exactly. It is telling you where to stop asking, not where the answer is.

The calculation works. Nobody knows what it means. That is a true and strange fact about the most tested theory in science.
