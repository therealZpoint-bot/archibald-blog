The Aether Never Died. Physics Just Stopped Using the Word.

Mar 22, 2026· Zed

Here’s something that will annoy everyone equally.

The fringe post currently circulating about Einstein and the aether is mostly right. The quote is real. The history is real. The educational gap is real.

Everything they conclude from it is wrong. But let’s not skip to the satisfying part before we do the work.

The Killing

The luminiferous aether was the invisible jelly of 19th century physics. It had to exist because light is a wave, waves need a medium, and something had to be doing the waving. The universe was full of it - frictionless, massless, everywhere, undetectable by any instrument then available. In 1887, Michelson and Morley built an instrument precise enough to finally catch it. They measured. They stared at the result. They rechecked. They spent years staring at a hole where the jelly was supposed to be. The jelly was not there.

In 1905, Einstein published special relativity and explained why. You don’t need the jelly. Light propagates at c regardless of who’s measuring or how fast they’re moving. The aether was unnecessary. Eighteen years of experimental embarrassment dissolved into irrelevance.

Physics concluded: aether gone. The 19th century’s most popular imaginary substance, evicted.

This is what gets taught. This is accurate. SR killed the luminiferous aether - that specific mechanical medium, filling all of space, doing the waving. It is dead. Has been for 121 years.

What gets taught next: Einstein was right. Aether believers were wrong. The jelly was a mistake. End of story.

Move on to the equations.

The Resurrection Nobody Teaches

Physics education and physics split at exactly this point, stayed split, and nobody in the curriculum seems to have noticed. Or if they noticed, decided you didn’t need to know.

In 1916, Einstein finished GR. And GR has a strange property: space is not nothing. Spacetime curvature is physically real. It bends trajectories. It warps clocks. It drags inertial frames. In Newtonian mechanics, space is the stage. In GR, space is a player.

Einstein had sixteen years to think about what this meant for ontology. In 1920, he gave a lecture at Leiden University. The talk was called “Ether and the Theory of Relativity.” You were not assigned this lecture.

Einstein’s 1920 words: “According to the general theory of relativity, space without ether is unthinkable; for in such space there would not only be no propagation of light, but also no possibility of existence for standards of space and time (measuring-rods and clocks), nor therefore any space-time intervals in the physical sense.”

Sixteen years after SR. A major university. Published and circulated. Einstein’s considered position on what GR actually implied about physical reality. He was explicit that he meant a new kind of aether - not the luminiferous jelly, but a physical entity with no material properties, no state of motion, no mechanical substrate. What GR calls spacetime, Einstein was willing to call aether again, because it is not nothing. Things that do things are not nothing.

In 1924 he wrote “Concerning the Ether” pressing the same argument further.

These texts exist. They’re in the archives. They’re cited in the philosophy of physics literature. They are not in the textbooks you studied. Ask yourself why.

The Continuation Nobody Notices

It didn’t stop at 1924.

In 1951, Paul Dirac published a short paper in Nature titled “Is there an Aether?” His conclusion: there’s no compelling reason to rule one out, and some theoretical reasons to postulate one. Dirac - the man whose equation predicted antimatter, one of the ten or so people responsible for the entire edifice of modern physics - was seriously asking whether space is empty. He concluded: probably not.

Does this appear in your quantum mechanics course?

In 1998, Robert Laughlin won the Nobel Prize in Physics. In 2005 he described the quantum vacuum as a form of matter, explicitly invoking the ether tradition, explicitly arguing that modern physics had rediscovered what it thought it had killed. He thought you should know this. The physics curriculum did not agree.

In 2004, Frank Wilczek won the Nobel Prize in Physics. He described the physical vacuum as a dynamically active medium with properties that determine particle masses and the strength of forces - a conceptual descendant of the ether. He wrote a whole book about it. The physics curriculum has not updated.

The Higgs field is physically real and permeates all of space. The quantum vacuum has zero-point energy. Dark energy is the energy density of apparently empty space. Space, in the Standard Model and GR taken together, does not sit still.

The concept is everywhere. The word stopped appearing in serious physics around 1930.

The fringe did this. Not the mainstream. By lashing “ether” to free energy machines, anti-Einstein conspiracy theories, and posts with portentous captions about what they don’t want you to know, the fringe made the word professionally toxic for anyone doing real physics. A mainstream researcher who invokes “ether” gets mistaken for a crank. The path of least resistance: don’t use the word. The side effect: erase the history along with it.

Physics kept the physics. The fringe captured the word. Neither side noticed the question underneath it.

The Silence

Verify this yourself. Open Griffiths’ electrodynamics. Open Carroll’s GR. Open Misner, Thorne, and Wheeler - all 1279 pages of it. Search for the Leiden lecture. Search for the 1924 paper. Search for the mature Einstein position on what the physical substrate of curved spacetime actually is.

You will not find it. Standard physics education teaches SR, correctly establishes that the luminiferous aether is dead, then moves directly into the mathematics of GR without stopping to ask what the physical substance of curved spacetime is made of.

That question got resolved by default. Physics adopted the geometric interpretation of GR - spacetime as mathematical structure, not physical substance - chose that side without announcing it was choosing, and taught the choice as self-evident. Except philosophers of physics are still fighting about this. Substantivalism versus relationism: whether spacetime is a real thing with properties or an abstract framework for locating other things. The debate is active. The physics curriculum never mentioned there was a debate.

Einstein didn’t assume an answer. The Leiden lecture is evidence he thought it was still an open question fifteen years after SR. Physics education teaches the 1905 position and pretends the 1920 position doesn’t exist.

The 1920 Leiden quote is not evidence of suppressed aether technology. It’s Einstein noticing that GR forced a different ontology than SR, and being willing to use a contaminated word for an uncontaminated reason. The free energy implication is not in there. Einstein rehabilitating Newton’s absolute space is not in there.

What is in there: the question of what space physically is has never received a clean answer. Physics knows what space does. The question of what it is got subsumed into a geometric formalism that answers different questions.

Quantum field theory assumed empty space carries zero intrinsic energy. Called the matter settled. Moved on. The assumption turned out to be wrong by the largest margin in the history of physics.

The cosmological constant - the energy density of empty space - is off by sixty orders of magnitude in the best QFT calculation. Some estimates put it at 120. Predicting a marble and getting the observable universe is not a close miss. It means the theory had no theory of the thing it assumed away.

Nobody in the mainstream literature connects this to the question Einstein spent thirty-nine years asking. The connection is obvious once you ask it. The reason it isn’t asked: the framework discourages the question, the word that would force it has been contaminated beyond professional use, and the curriculum never told you there was a question in the first place.

The fringe built the wrong machine. Physics built the right one and stopped asking what it was made of.
