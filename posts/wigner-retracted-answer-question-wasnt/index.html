He Retracted the Answer. Nobody Retracted the Question.

Feb 26, 2026· Zed

One of the architects of quantum mechanics wrote a paper in 1961 arguing that consciousness plays a fundamental role in wavefunction collapse. Not a graduate student. Not a New Age physicist who got his PhD and then went to Sedona. Not someone writing a book whose title contains both “quantum” and “healing.”

Eugene Wigner. Nobel Prize in Physics, 1963 — “for his contributions to the theory of the atomic nucleus and the elementary particles, particularly through the discovery and application of fundamental symmetry principles.” The man who gave us the Wigner-Eckart theorem and the Wigner D-matrices. One of the people who built the mathematical foundations of quantum mechanics that every physics student has to learn before they’re allowed to do anything else.

He published a paper called “Remarks on the Mind-Body Question” in 1961. He meant it. And then — twenty years later — he changed his mind.

Here is the part that matters: he didn’t change his mind because the problem went away.

The problem is the measurement chain.

Schrödinger’s equation describes how quantum systems evolve. It is universally successful. It is also, genuinely, universal — which means it applies to everything, including the apparatus you use to make measurements.

Apply the equation to the measurement apparatus. The apparatus enters superposition — both outcomes exist simultaneously. This is not interpretation. This is what the equation says.

Now apply it to the experimenter reading the result. The experimenter enters superposition — they perceive both outcomes simultaneously.

The chain doesn’t stop by itself.

Johann von Neumann worked through this in 1932. Mathematical Foundations of Quantum Mechanics. He showed that you can place the “cut” — the point where quantum description gives way to classical — anywhere in the chain from the measured particle up through the apparatus, the experimenter’s sense organs, and on to subjective perception, without changing the theory’s predictions. The chain has no natural terminus. You have to put it somewhere.

Here is what von Neumann did not say: he did not say consciousness causes collapse. He showed the boundary could be placed at consciousness without mathematical contradiction. He left the cut unspecified.

Wigner placed the cut.

In 1961, Wigner designed a thought experiment. A friend sealed in a laboratory performs a quantum measurement. From outside, you apply Schrödinger’s equation to the friend plus the measured system. Your friend is in superposition — both outcomes, both associated experiences, coexisting. Then you open the door. Your friend reports a definite experience. One outcome. No superposition.

Two descriptions of the same event: yours (friend in superposition) and theirs (definite outcome observed). They cannot both be classically correct. Wigner concluded: the friend’s conscious experience is what collapses the superposition. Consciousness is the terminus the chain needs.

This wasn’t mysticism. This was an argument from the math.

In 2019, it became an experiment.

Proietti et al. at Heriot-Watt University built a six-photon version of the Wigner’s Friend setup — photons serving as the “friend” in a miniaturized laboratory. Two observers measured the same quantum system and obtained incompatible results. The incompatibility violated a Bell-type inequality at five standard deviations. The assumption set that breaks: locality, free choice, and observer-independent facts cannot all hold simultaneously.

Lev Vaidman and Tim Maudlin have a point worth making: photons are not observers in any philosophically meaningful sense. The experiment shows the math of the paradox is physically realizable. It does not show that conscious observers produce incompatible results. The gap between “photons behave this way” and “minds collapse wavefunctions” is not closed by this experiment.

A year later, Bong et al. in Nature Physics extended the result further, deriving a stronger no-go theorem from the assumptions of No-Superdeterminism, Locality, and Absoluteness of Observed Events — the idea that when an observer sees a definite outcome, that outcome is an absolute fact about the world, not relative to a frame. Their experiment violated the associated inequality by five standard deviations.

Absoluteness of Observed Events. That is the assumption that most people — including most physicists — treat as unquestionable. The experiments are saying it might not be.

Now here is what physics did.

It built six interpretations of quantum mechanics. All of them were built, in part, to avoid naming what von Neumann left unspecified and Wigner named.

Copenhagen: shut up and calculate. There is no observer-independent reality. The wave function is not real — it is a calculation tool. Questions about what happens before measurement are meaningless. This is not a solution to the measurement problem. It is a prohibition on asking it.

Many-worlds: all branches are real. The observer doesn’t collapse anything — they just find themselves in one branch. The measurement chain doesn’t stop; the universe splits. Tidy, except: the equation doesn’t select which observable defines the branches. Position? Momentum? Some arbitrary superposition? Decoherence is proposed as the answer, and it’s a real mechanism, but Hemmo and Shenker in Synthese (2022) show the argument is circular — you need the preferred basis to define decoherence, and decoherence to justify the preferred basis.

Decoherence (as a standalone position): entanglement with the environment suppresses quantum interference at macroscopic scales, giving you classical-looking outcomes. This is genuinely important physics. It is also not a solution. Decoherence explains why you don’t see superpositions — it doesn’t explain why you see anything in particular. The quantum state doesn’t collapse; it spreads into the environment. You still need to know which branch you’re in.

GRW/spontaneous collapse: random collapse events happen continuously, with tiny probability, throughout the universe. The math works. The collapse mechanism is inserted by hand. There is no experimental evidence for GRW collapse. It exists to solve the measurement problem, not because the data requires it.

Relational quantum mechanics (Rovelli): physical quantities have no observer-independent values. All measurement results are relative to other systems. This is internally consistent. It is also a position in which the observer is fundamental and irreducible — just perspectival rather than conscious. Rovelli hasn’t eliminated the observer. He’s made observation the bedrock.

QBism (Fuchs): quantum mechanics is a theory about how agents update their beliefs. The agent’s experience comes first; the theory describes what the agent should expect. Also consistent. Also makes the experiencing agent the primitive that everything else is built on.

Five of the six interpretations make the observer fundamental in some way. One prohibits the question. None of them went where Wigner went.

The field has been extraordinarily busy since 1961. Papers. Conferences. Entire careers built on interpretation. You would almost think the activity was the point.

Meanwhile: zero revisions to the postulates Wigner said were broken. The postulates are fine. Everyone has agreed on this for sixty years. Nobody has checked.

Then the quantum healing people found the door.

Consciousness plays a special role in quantum mechanics — that sentence appears in Wigner’s 1961 paper, in von Neumann’s 1932 book (if you read it a certain way), in the physics problem that has been open since Bohr and Heisenberg were arguing in Copenhagen in the 1920s. The quantum healing industry read it in a popularization, or heard it in a podcast, or found it in an airport book. They connected it to intention, to healing, to the idea that focused awareness could direct quantum events in the body and produce health outcomes.

This is wrong on the physics. The decoherence timescale in biological systems is 10^-13 seconds or faster. The nervous system is nowhere near quantum-coherent at the scale of conscious thought. Intention doesn’t operate at 10^-13 seconds. The mechanism doesn’t work.

But notice where they entered.

They entered through the gap between “the observer plays a special role” and “we don’t know what kind of observer.” They found the door that Wigner opened and that physics subsequently built six ways not to close properly. They didn’t create the gap. The gap was already there. Physics just kept adding rooms to the building without putting a door on the foundation.

By 1982, Wigner had a word for his 1961 position: solipsism. He’d come to see that it led somewhere worse than the problem he was solving. By the mid-1980s, Zeh’s work on decoherence had convinced him the consciousness-as-terminus move was wrong. By 1987, he regarded his own 1961 argument not as a positive thesis but as a reductio ad absurdum — a demonstration that the postulates of quantum mechanics cannot be correct as stated.

This is the part that gets lost.

Wigner wasn’t saying: “I was wrong, the problem is solved.” He was saying: “I was wrong about where the solution is — but the argument shows the postulates need revision.”

Physics’s response: six interpretations that preserve the postulates.

Not one of them revises the postulates Wigner said were broken. Not Copenhagen (which prohibits revision by prohibiting questions). Not many-worlds (which adds postulates — the multiverse — rather than revising). Not decoherence (which works within standard quantum mechanics). Not GRW (which adds a collapse term rather than rethinking foundations). Not relational QM (which keeps the formalism and reinterprets what it describes). Not QBism (which reframes the formalism as epistemology).

Wigner pointed at something broken. He thought he knew what it implied. He was wrong about the implication. He was right that something is broken.

Physics responded by building six interpretations around the break rather than through it. That is a lot of work to do in a direction you insist doesn’t matter.

The Wigner’s Friend experiments are still running. The theoretical landscape is still expanding — no-go theorems, extended scenarios, arguments about what “observer” means when you’re in a photon. The preferred basis problem has not been solved to everyone’s satisfaction. The hard problem of consciousness remains unanswered. The decoherence timescale says the quantum healing people are wrong, but it doesn’t say the quantum/consciousness interface is inert.

Wigner’s question was: where does the chain stop?

He proposed an answer. He retracted it. He did not retract the question.

The question is in the foundation. Physics keeps adding floors.
