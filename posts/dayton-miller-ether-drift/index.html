The Official Explanation Came Fourteen Years After He Died. Nobody Has Since Run the Experiment.

Mar 22, 2026· Zed

Physics has a clean story about ether drift. Michelson and Morley ran the experiment in 1887, found nothing, and special relativity became possible. One experiment, one null result, one conclusion. The null result is famous. It’s in every textbook. The story ends there.

What the textbooks skip: Michelson spent the next four decades running the same experiment anyway. He kept looking for ether drift through 1929, two years before his death, including a final series at Mount Wilson with Dayton Miller. Miller ran the experiments Michelson wanted to run but couldn’t: higher altitude, larger interferometer, more observations. Over thirty years, Miller accumulated the largest interferometer dataset of its type ever assembled. At Mount Wilson in 1925 and 1926 - four experimental epochs across two years - he ran 316 sets of observations.

He found a non-zero signal. Consistently. Across all four epochs. Approximately 10 km/s drift.

Miller wasn’t a crank. He was a Fellow of the National Academy of Sciences. He built the largest gas-mode interferometer ever constructed: 4.3 meters across, a 64-meter reflected light path. His experimental design was deliberate - he worked at altitude (Mount Wilson sits at 1,740 meters) because his specific hypothesis about ether behavior predicted altitude-dependent effects. A lower-altitude experiment wasn’t a replication of his claim. A smaller apparatus wasn’t a replication of his method. He knew this and said so.

His 1933 paper in Reviews of Modern Physics ran 40 pages. Not a short letter, not a conference abstract - forty pages, full methodology, raw data, all four epochs, complete analysis. Miller knew the challenge would come eventually. His closing statement reads like a man writing in advance of his own prosecution: “All of the original observations have been included in the calculation, without any omissions and without the assignment of weights. No corrections of any kind have been applied to the observed quantities.”

He documented everything. He excluded nothing. He adjusted nothing.

If the Earth moves through ether at 30 km/s, the expected fringe shift corresponds to a roughly 30 km/s signal. Miller measured approximately 10. That’s not zero. Ten kilometers per second of consistent, altitude-detected drift across 316 observation sets, two years, four epochs - and the official verdict is that nothing was found. An anomaly requiring explanation got reclassified as a null result. These are not the same thing.

In May 1921, Albert Einstein visited Princeton for the first time. During a conversation with the mathematician Oswald Veblen, he said: “Raffiniert ist der Herrgott, aber boshaft ist er nicht” - “Subtle is the Lord God, but malicious He is not.” He was making a remark about his inability to believe in ether-drift experiments, including some preliminary work by Miller.

That was 1921. Miller’s main dataset - the 316 observation sets across four epochs at Mount Wilson - didn’t exist until 1925 and 1926.

Einstein had aesthetically dismissed the experiments four years before the evidence was in. He later described the phrase as “an expression for my unbelief in the interpretation of some experiments about the ’ether-wind.’” It’s inscribed on the fireplace at Princeton’s Fine Hall. It’s in every physics retrospective. It’s the epitaph on the ether-drift question.

He said it before he’d seen the data.

In July 1925, with the Mount Wilson results now in hand, Einstein wrote publicly: “Should the positive result be confirmed, then the special theory of relativity and with it the general theory of relativity, in its current form, would be invalid.” Cautious. Appropriate.

In December 1925, he wrote privately to his friend Michele Besso: “I didn’t take them seriously for a single moment.”

In January 1926, he sent a cable to a journalist: “There is virtually no chance that Miller’s experiments were correct.”

The public letter says: if he’s right, the whole theory falls. The private letter says: I didn’t take it seriously for a single moment. The cable to the press says: virtually no chance he’s right.

The contemporaneous replications, if you’re feeling generous with the word:

Kennedy (1926) found null results. Different apparatus. Different site. Illingworth (1927) found null results. Modified Kennedy apparatus. Different site. Joos (1930) found null results. Jena, Germany, sea level, different construction.

Call these replications if you want. Miller’s altitude-dependent hypothesis specifically predicted that sea-level experiments would find weaker or null results. His hypothesis wasn’t that ether drift exists everywhere equally - it was that the effect is altitude-dependent, and that experiments at sea level would systematically undercount it. Sea-level null results weren’t a refutation. They were exactly what his hypothesis predicted. Citing them as replications for the next seventy years isn’t sloppy scholarship. It’s something worse.

Dayton Miller died on February 22, 1941.

Fourteen years later - 1955 - Robert Shankland, S. W. McCuskey, F. C. Leone, and G. Kuerti published “New Analysis of the Interferometer Observations of Dayton C. Miller” in Reviews of Modern Physics, Vol. 27, No. 2, pp. 167–178.

Same journal. Same type of experiment. Different conclusion.

Let’s be precise about who was doing this.

Shankland took Miller’s endowed chair at Case in 1941, the year Miller died. Same position, same department, same building. The reanalysis of a dead man’s life’s work was not handed to an independent team at another institution, not assigned to anyone without a stake in the outcome - it was entrusted to the man who inherited everything Miller left behind.

And Einstein didn’t just tolerate the project. He wrote to Shankland in 1954 with active support for the planned reanalysis. The man whose entire theoretical structure required Miller to be wrong was personally encouraging the project that would declare Miller wrong.

This is the institutional arrangement that produced the official verdict. Miller’s data. His successor at his own institution. Einstein’s blessing. The experiment itself was not repeated.

Shankland’s team concluded Miller’s positive signal was caused by statistical fluctuations from imprecise fringe readings and local temperature gradients specific to Mount Wilson’s open-air setup. The thermal explanation. Error, not signal.

Miller could not respond. He had been dead for fourteen years.

The thermal explanation has not been independently verified.

I looked. The only other published examination I could find is Thomas Roberts in 2006 - a preprint, not peer-reviewed, arXiv:physics/0608238 - and Roberts concluded Miller’s results weren’t valid either, but for entirely different reasons. He argued statistical artifact, not temperature gradients. Two incompatible explanations that happen to land on the same verdict. Call that verification if the word still means something to you. Neither theory has been tested against Miller’s original apparatus, which no longer exists.

Modern laser interferometry produces null results at many orders of magnitude better sensitivity than anything Miller could have measured. It also doesn’t replicate Miller’s specific physical claim. These experiments operate in vacuum, at sea level, using different physics. Miller’s hypothesis was about altitude-dependent effects in open-air gas-mode apparatus. A vacuum interferometer at sea level doesn’t test that. It tests something else, more precisely. The modern null is not a replication. It’s a better answer to a different question.

The question Miller spent thirty years on was officially closed in 1955 by a data re-analysis. The analyst was his institutional successor. The project was encouraged by the person whose career most required a specific outcome. The experiment was not repeated. The thermal explanation has never been independently examined. Shankland’s paper was published in a flagship journal, cited hundreds of times, and became the permanent response to anyone who asks about Miller’s results - a pronouncement issued over a dead man’s data, mistaken ever since for an experimental result.

Nobody asked who was examining the examiners.
