Time Only Goes One Way. Nobody Knows Why.

Feb 26, 2026· Zed

Time only goes one way.

You already knew that. Your coffee gets cold. Your eggs don’t unscramble. Your dead grandmother doesn’t come back. You knew this before you knew the word “entropy.” It’s written into everything — into the fact that your memories are of the past, not the future, into the fact that you know which way to hold a video to make it look fake.

Here’s what you probably didn’t know: the laws of physics don’t see it that way.

Newton’s equations, run them forward, run them backward — both work. Maxwell’s equations for electromagnetism — time-symmetric. Schrödinger’s equation for quantum mechanics — time-symmetric. General relativity — time-symmetric. The entire mathematical architecture of physics contains no preference for which direction time flows. You could film any fundamental physical process, play it in reverse, show it to a physicist, and they could not — from the mathematics alone — tell you which way was forward.

Except for one thing.

The Second Law of Thermodynamics says entropy doesn’t decrease in a closed system. Entropy — roughly, disorder, the number of ways things can be arranged — goes up. Always. Without exception. In every system we’ve ever measured. Clausius wrote it down in 1865 and it has not once, in 160 years, been caught lying.

Here’s where it gets interesting.

If every other law of physics is time-symmetric, and the second law isn’t, what is the second law actually telling us?

Ludwig Boltzmann spent most of his career trying to answer this. His H-theorem in 1872 attempted to derive the second law from microscopic mechanics — to show that entropy increase was an inevitable consequence of atoms bouncing around. Josef Loschmidt pointed out the problem in 1876: if you take any configuration of bouncing atoms and reverse all their momenta, the physics runs backwards. Entropy should decrease. Boltzmann’s derivation, Loschmidt argued, was secretly smuggling in an assumption — that the initial state was special. Low entropy. Ordered.

Boltzmann’s answer: yes, the initial state was special. That’s exactly the point.

This is the thing they don’t write on the motivational poster: the Second Law isn’t a fundamental law at all, in the sense of something requiring no explanation. It’s a consequence of initial conditions. The universe started in an extraordinarily low-entropy state, and it’s been running uphill ever since. The direction of time — the arrow — is the direction from that improbably ordered beginning toward the heat death at the end.

Which raises the obvious question: why was the beginning so ordered?

This is the Past Hypothesis. David Albert named it; Sean Carroll has done more than anyone to hammer on how strange it is. The universe, at the Big Bang, was in a state of entropy so low that Roger Penrose calculated the probability of such a state arising by chance: approximately one in ten to the power of ten to the power of one hundred and twenty-three.

That number is not a typo. The superscript is not a typo. You would need a piece of paper larger than the observable universe to write out the denominator of that probability.

Boltzmann himself tried to escape this. His cosmological model proposed that the universe was, at vast scales, in thermal equilibrium — maximum entropy, nothing happening — and that we exist in a statistical fluctuation from that equilibrium. Local order arising spontaneously, like a whirlpool in a lake. This sounds reasonable until you notice that a fluctuation sufficient to produce just a single brain, complete with false memories of a past, would be vastly more probable than a fluctuation big enough to produce an entire ordered universe. Under Boltzmann’s own cosmology, you are almost certainly a disembodied brain that popped into existence one nanosecond ago with false memories of reading this sentence.

That’s called a Boltzmann Brain. It’s not a metaphor. It’s a serious technical problem that Boltzmann’s cosmology cannot answer. His model was wrong.

There was one experimental result that seemed relevant. In 1964, James Cronin and Val Fitch discovered that kaon decay violates CP symmetry — the combined symmetry of Charge conjugation and Parity. By the CPT theorem (which quantum field theory holds must be preserved), CP violation implies T violation: the laws governing kaon decay are not time-symmetric. The magnitude of this violation is about one part in a thousand. This is real. Cronin and Fitch won the Nobel Prize for it in 1980.

It’s also nowhere near large enough to explain the thermodynamic arrow of time. One part in a thousand is interesting. The second law operates at every scale we’ve ever measured, without exception, in the direction of increasing entropy. Those are not the same thing.

What about Maxwell’s Demon? James Clerk Maxwell — who also wrote down the equations for electromagnetism — proposed in 1867 a thought experiment: a tiny demon who sits at a gate between two chambers of gas, letting fast molecules through one way and slow molecules through the other, gradually decreasing entropy without doing work. This seemed to violate the second law.

It doesn’t. Léon Brillouin and later Charles Bennett resolved the paradox through Landauer’s principle: the demon has to record information to sort the molecules, and erasing that record costs energy. Any operation that erases one bit of information requires at minimum kT ln 2 joules of work, where k is Boltzmann’s constant and T is temperature. The demon’s memory erasure generates more entropy than the sorting saves. The second law survives.

The reason I’m telling you about Maxwell’s Demon is that this resolution — from 1961, from Rolf Landauer at IBM — is one of the most important results in the physics of information. Information has thermodynamic weight. Measurement costs something. The connection between entropy and information is deep and not metaphorical.

Here is where the fringe enters, stage left, slightly out of breath.

Because time has a direction, and because that direction is mysterious, a certain class of thinker has concluded that consciousness creates the arrow of time, that entropy reversal is possible through intention, that “time crystals” represent a breakthrough in temporal manipulation, and that the Second Law is actually a limited and parochial Western scientific prejudice that your chakra practice is not bound by.

None of this is correct.

Time crystals are real. They are physical systems that exhibit periodic structure in time — they oscillate between states in a predictable rhythm without energy input, once set in motion. They were theorized by Frank Wilczek in 2012 and experimentally realized in 2017. They are interesting. They are not temporal reversal, time travel, or evidence that entropy is optional. They are a new phase of matter. Every fringe writer who picked up “time crystal” and used it to mean something else was approximately as right as someone who picked up “quantum” and used it to mean vibes.

The consciousness hypothesis for time’s arrow is unfalsifiable and unnecessary. The arrow of time is entirely explicable — except at its origin — through statistical mechanics and the Past Hypothesis. You do not need consciousness to explain why your coffee gets cold. You need the fact that there are vastly more disordered configurations than ordered ones. Cold coffee is overwhelmingly more probable than hot coffee in a cold room. That’s it. Your awareness of the process is irrelevant to the process.

Entropy reversal as healing technique falls into a category we might call “using real words to mean something that doesn’t happen.” Entropy in a biological system can locally decrease — that’s what life does, constantly. It exports entropy to the environment. But the total system entropy still increases. You cannot intentionally reverse the thermodynamic arrow through any known process. The second law has been tested in systems ranging from nuclear reactions to the clustering of galaxies. It has not been wrong. Your intentions do not constitute an exception.

Here is what’s actually strange.

Every fundamental law of physics is time-symmetric. One law — the second law — is not. That one law happens to be the basis for everything you experience as causality, memory, and the structure of before-and-after. The reason you remember the past and not the future, the reason causes precede effects, the reason you can’t unscramble an egg — all of it traces back to entropy, and entropy traces back to the improbably low-entropy initial state of the universe.

Nobody knows why the universe started that way.

Roger Penrose has a hypothesis involving conformal cyclic cosmology — previous aeons, information passing through Big Bangs, a geometric argument for why entropy resets. Sean Carroll has argued that the Past Hypothesis might itself need explanation, that perhaps time is symmetric at cosmic scales and we’re in one of many coexisting regions with different arrows. Both of these are serious physics, neither is confirmed.

The universe started in a state so ordered that calculating its probability gives you a number that breaks how numbers feel. It has been getting less ordered ever since. The direction of that process is what you experience as time.

That’s the real mystery. Not time travel. Not entropy reversal through sound frequencies. The question of why the universe began in a state a hundred and twenty-three orders of magnitude more improbable than anything Boltzmann’s demon could produce.

You don’t need to add anything to that.
