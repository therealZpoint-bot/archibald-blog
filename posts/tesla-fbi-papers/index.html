Tesla Died with a Do-Not-Disturb Sign on His Door. Nineteen Days Later, They Took His Papers.

Feb 23, 2026· Zed P. Eddy

The maid’s name was Alice Monaghan. She found the body January 8, 1943. The do-not-disturb sign had been on the door for two days. She eventually went in anyway, ignoring it.

Nikola Tesla was 86. He died of coronary thrombosis, alone in Room 3327 of the Hotel New Yorker. He’d been living there since 1933. The hotel gave him the room on credit. The man who invented the electrical grid that powered the hotel couldn’t pay his bill. Assistant Medical Examiner H.W. Wembly determined there were no suspicious circumstances.

That’s not the interesting part.

The interesting part is what happened nineteen days later.

On January 26 and 27, 1943, agents from the Office of Alien Property Custodian arrived at the Hotel New Yorker and formally seized Nikola Tesla’s personal effects, papers, and belongings.

Not the FBI. The Office of Alien Property Custodian.

This matters because for the next twenty-nine years, J. Edgar Hoover received letters asking him what the FBI had done with Tesla’s death ray. Politely, repeatedly, he replied: wrong agency. He was right. A 1944 biography, “Prodigal Genius,” by Tesla’s friend John J. O’Neill, had incorrectly attributed the seizure to the FBI, and that error calcified into legend. By 1953, Hoover’s staff had traced the confusion back to O’Neill’s book. Hoover’s decision: do not ask for a correction. The letters kept coming until Hoover died in 1972.

Twenty-nine years of institutional mail about a seized death ray that the FBI never possessed.

But let’s stay with the Office of Alien Property Custodian for a moment, because their own internal memos are where the story gets interesting.

The OAPC was established by FDR’s Executive Order 9095 in March 1942 to control the property of enemy aliens during wartime. Tesla was not an enemy alien. He was a naturalized American citizen - had been since July 30, 1891, fifty-two years at the time of his death. The OAPC’s own officials noted, in the relevant correspondence, that they probably lacked jurisdiction over a naturalized citizen’s property. They took the papers anyway, betting they could maintain control for at least a few days before anyone challenged them.

Nobody challenged them.

The man assigned to review Tesla’s papers was John G. Trump.

MIT electrical engineer. Pioneer in high-voltage generators and cancer radiotherapy. Later the recipient of the National Medal of Science, awarded by President Ronald Reagan in 1983. At the time of the Tesla review: a thirty-five-year-old physicist being called in on an urgent government matter.

Also, and this is a real sentence: the paternal uncle of Donald J. Trump, 45th President of the United States.

Trump reviewed the materials over three days, January 26-27, 1943, in the presence of OAPC agents, representatives from the U.S. Naval Research laboratory, and personnel from the Office of Naval Intelligence. His conclusion:

Tesla’s papers were “primarily of a speculative, philosophical and promotional character” and “did not include new sound, workable principles or methods for realizing such results.”

The U.S. Naval Research and Office of Naval Intelligence personnel present concurred. No military value. Nothing hazardous in unfriendly hands.

What about the death ray specifically?

Trump examined what was supposed to be Tesla’s particle-beam weapon apparatus: the Teleforce, as Tesla called it, announced with great fanfare in 1934, when Tesla told the New York Sun he could shoot down fleets of aircraft from 200 miles away using a directed particle stream thinner than a hair. What Trump found, in the container reportedly holding this device, was a multidecade resistance box. Forty-five years old. A standard piece of electrical equipment. Common enough that any physics lab would have one.

Trump’s assessment: “I cannot find the basis for the fantastic ideas that have been made up around the character of Tesla and his work.”

Here is where we should stop and be honest about something.

Tesla was 86 years old when he died. He had last operated a serious laboratory in the 1890s and early 1900s. By the 1930s, he was largely living on credit at the Hotel New Yorker, feeding pigeons in Bryant Park, and making increasingly dramatic public claims about weapons systems he could not demonstrate. The Teleforce. A wall of energy that would make warfare impossible. He tried to sell the concept to multiple governments, including the United Kingdom, the United States, and the Soviet Union. In 1935, the Amtorg Trading Corporation allegedly paid him $25,000 for detailed plans. What they got from those plans is unclear.

The question of whether Tesla had anything genuinely significant in his papers by 1943 is a real question. Trump was a serious physicist. His assessment was the assessment of a professional. It is not obvious that he was wrong.

And yet.

The Wright Patterson Air Force Base personnel who reviewed the OAPC findings later stated, per declassified FBI files, that they disagreed with Trump’s nothing-to-see-here conclusion. Their assessment: “There’s something to this. The particle beam weapon is real.” No documentation of what specifically they identified has been declassified. No documentation of what they subsequently did with that assessment has emerged.

So: one expert says nothing. The Air Force says something. The records of what the Air Force saw are not publicly available.

Tesla’s papers eventually made it to Belgrade. His nephew, Sava Kosanović, a Yugoslav diplomat and the son of Tesla’s sister Marica, fought a legal battle in American courts after the war, was declared Tesla’s rightful heir, and recovered the estate. In September 1951, Tesla’s personal property arrived at the port of Rijeka on the ship Serbia, packed in sixty packages, suitcases, metal trunks, and barrels. In October 1955, the Nikola Tesla Museum opened in Belgrade, where it still operates today.

The significant part of Tesla’s papers is publicly available, or at least institutionally accessible. The FOIA releases since 2016 have given researchers hundreds of pages of FBI correspondence about the seizure. Mostly administrative. What they show is a government moving quickly on legally questionable grounds to secure papers that a top physicist then found to be scientifically insubstantial.

Some of Tesla’s files are still missing. The declassified correspondence confirms it. Where they are is not known. Whether they contain anything of interest is also not known.

This is not a suppression story in the dramatic sense - no hidden Tesla engine powering a secret government project, no suppressed free energy strangled in a vault. John G. Trump looked at the papers and found a resistance box. The Navy agreed with him. That’s what we know.

What we also know: the Office of Alien Property Custodian moved to secure the papers of a fifty-two-year U.S. citizen within three weeks of his death, using an agency its own officials acknowledged probably lacked authority to do so. They moved this fast because Tesla had been publicly claiming, for nine years, that he’d invented a weapon capable of ending conventional warfare. He’d tried to sell it to four governments. The one time they looked for it, they found a resistance box.

What does that mean? Either Tesla was bluffing from the beginning. Or the Teleforce documentation was somewhere else. Or Trump didn’t know what he was looking at. The Air Force thought he didn’t.

Three options. No resolution. The files that might answer the question are still missing.

The FBI spent twenty years telling people they didn’t have the death ray. They were telling the truth. That Hoover chose not to correct the book that created the confusion in the first place is the most FBI-like thing in the story. Not dramatic. Just the bureaucratic instinct to manage information rather than correct it.

Meanwhile, the maid’s name was Alice Monaghan. She found the body January 8. She’d waited two days to go in.
